Introduction


Hello there, welcome to this introductory page for observing the stars and other celestial objects in the Sky. I won’t focus upon the history of observation for too long, however it is always a good thing to start from the very beginning.

Well before our time, with powerful technology and giant telescopes, ancient civilisations were already looking at the Sky in the attempt to observe, map and predict the movements of celestial objects. How long ago? As early as the Babylonians and the Greek, 200-300 B.C.

There is not a defined date when humans started looking critically at the Sky but many manufacts still happen to be present today, and they show remarkable inventions and ingenuity. For example, the Antikythera mechanism, design by Hipparchus is a spectacular example of ancient technology. As many describe it as an early analogue computer capable of calculating the location of the Sun, Moon and planets for a given date. The Greek didn’t just do that, as you can notice by first looking through a manual of astronomy, most of the names of constellations and stars in the Northern Hemisphere have a greek root, such as Procyon (in Canis Minor constellation) which literally translates with “Before the dog”. You can also notice a lot of Roman influence, meaning Latin Roman, in lots of the names as well as the stories and legends that surround constellations.

For instance, Andromeda which is a constellation and also a galaxy, the only astronomical object outside our galaxy (the Milky Way) that can be seen with the naked eye. The mythological figure of a chained virgin, daughter of king Cepheus and queen Cassiopeia (also constellations in the northern sky) left on the rocks to be sacrificed to the sea monster sent by Poseidon (the god of the Seas) as a punishment for her mother’s vanity. Everybody knows the name Perseus who killed one of the Gorgons, Medusa, cut her head off and used it as a weapon to defeat the monster and save the virgin.

This kind of elaborate stories are extremely fascinating and interesting but also well thought out. There is countless belonging to the Mediterranean civilisations, and you could just get lost by learning about all of them.

One of the biggest revolutions in astronomy was, without a doubt, the creation of the telescope by a scientist named Galileo Galilei. Why is that important? Well, for the first time in centuries, human could observe objects in the Sky that were previously invisible, and also could see planets more clearly. As a matter of fact, Galileo discovered Jupiter’s moons and also came to develop the most revolutionary theory in astronomy at the time. The Earth is not the centre of the Universe, but the Sun is, and planets rotate around it. This shift is well-known to students all around the world as the heliocentric theory (or Copernican Model of the Solar System) opposed to the Ptolemaic Model, or geocentric. At the time Galileo lived in Pisa, Italy, and the church, the Vatican had an enormous power upon the people. Such a theory would see Earth not as a gift of God or the most important among the other celestial objects, but just one of many. They couldn’t let it happen, and so Galileo was forced (under torture) to reject his theory.

Many years have passed but his contributions, as well as those of many other scientists, astronomers and keen passionate observers led to what we have now, comprised of 88 constellations, the knowledge that the Earth is a planet with 7 others rotating around a common star, conventionally and historically called Sun, to form a system by the name, very creative, of Solar System. This system travels through space around a centre which is not quite understandable at first, of a giant cluster of stars, planets, nebulas and other celestial objects, namely a galaxy. We found out that there are many more like our galaxy, many many more, and they could potentially host life as Earth does in our system. This is quite scary but also extremely exciting in my opinion.

Nowadays, technology has given us powerful computers capable of calculating and creating complex models of astronomical objects, the physics has improved as well to explain such objects, for example Black Holes. Today, we have a plethora of tools to observe the deep sky, gigantic telescopes as those in the Mauna Kea Observatory (Hawaii) able to look further, clearer and longer at objects. But also telescopes outside the atmosphere, such as the Hubble Telescope, which donated some stunning pictures of our Universe. Why are orbital telescopes better? For starters, there is no atmosphere out there at 540 km away, and when you’ll start observing you’ll notice that the atmosphere is kind of an obstacle, especially if you live in a foggy or polluted kind of place. Secondly, it is always dark. The Sun and Moon phases are not a problem as we face here on Earth. You might think this is trivial, and for backyard observation this actually is. However, as the laws of optics dictate, the longer the exposure the more the light a lens can “see”. On Earth, a day, as short as it can be, allows 12h, sometimes a bit longer, of continuous exposure. Is it enough? Not really because other than that the Earth moves, so the telescope won’t be fixed on an object for very long. On the other hand, high up in the atmosphere there is no “day” and most importantly the rotation of a satellite around Earth is somewhat arbitrary, which means that it can be changed to remain fixed on a point for a very long time. One of the most famous images from Hubble Telescope was taken looking at a portion of the sky for 3 days straight. It goes without saying you can’t do that on Earth.

So, why have I told you all this stuff? In my opinion, it is important when approaching a subject, such as astronomy, or chemistry or anything else, to be aware of the history behind it. To know what other people did before us and find out that fundamentally they weren’t so different. The feeling you get by looking through a telescope lens and watch a clear, hopefully, image of Jupiter or Saturn’s rings, and think that 400 years ago Galileo Galilei was doing the same helps me connecting at an emotional level, not a rational one, with one of my heroes, or for you it might be someone you think of with respect and awe.

I hope you understand that what I wrote here was a mere .0001% of things that could be said, seen or written about this subject. If you are interested, I encourage you to explore this topic even more and I can assure you will have a fantastic experience. The feeling of discovering the Universe is such a vast and immense place might make some of you feel tiny and alone, but it is actually the opposite. There is so much to see, explore, create and know. Also, in such a vast space we are definitely not alone.

Now that we got that out of the way, let’s proceed with some general concepts to help you start observing the sky.



Nomenclature


As you go through this website or other manuals, set of instructions etc you might come across certain words and terms you are not totally familiar with. Things that might mean something different than what is commonly known, so it is best to get a general understanding of the pseudo-scientific jargon that surrounds this subject.


Apparent Magnitude

The apparent magnitude of a celestial object refers to its relative brightness. In simple terms, magnitude depends on the object’s luminosity (which indicates the energy it radiates), and its distance form Earth. This last aspect is quite important. As a matter of fact, there are 2 types of magnitude: Absolute magnitude and Apparent Magnitude.

They both refers to a star’s brightness but in the case of absolute magnitude, this would be measured from a fixed distance from Earth. This distance is standard and common to every object at 10 parsecs (32,6 lightyears). Basically, it indicates what would be the magnitude of that object be at 10 parsecs from Earth. On the other hand, apparent magnitude is measured on the actual distance and brightness of the object as seen from Earth.

It is very important to keep in mind that the lower the magnitude the higher the brightness. This is due to historical reasons (Ptolemy used to classify the most visible stars in the night sky as 1st magnitude, because they were the ones you could see first, up to 6th magnitude, which represent the dimmest objects) as well as mathematical reasons since the magnitude scale is measured on a reverse logarithmic trend. This trend gives the actual ratio between objects’ magnitude. It is at 1001/5 which corresponds to 2.512. This means that a a star of magnitude 2.0 is 2.512 times brighter than a star of magnitude 3.0, 6.31 times brighter than a star of magnitude 4.0, and 100 times brighter than one of magnitude 7.0.

The visibility of astronomical objects by the naked eye can go up to magnitude of +6,5 in a very clear sky. Telescopes such as Hubble can see up to magnitude of +30.


Circumpolar

A circumpolar star is a star, as viewed from a given latitude on Earth, that never sets below the horizon due to its apparent proximity to one of the celestial poles. Circumpolar stars are therefore visible from said location toward the nearest pole for the entire night on every night of the year.

Whether a given star is circumpolar at the observer's latitude (θ) may be calculated in terms of the star's declination (δ). The star is circumpolar if θ + δ is greater than +90° (observer in Northern Hemisphere), or θ + δ is less than −90° (observer in Southern Hemisphere).


Light-Years

This one should be common knowledge especially for those science-fiction aficionados but it is a pivoting aspect of astronomy. First of all, it is a unit of length, as meters, or miles if you are North American, not of time as it is sometime mistakenly used. As the name suggests it is a simple multiplication of years and light, in this case it means the speed of light which is measured in km/s. The final product is expressed in kilometers or meters, but you don’t really want to see that in km because it’s quite long and from a human perspective it doesn’t really make any sense. In an average lifespan of 80 years, you might travel couple of millions of kilometers. One light-year is 9.46 trillion kilometers. As you can see it is not something for us to grasp, this is why we use light-years for astronomical distances. They become way more manageable. After all, the concept become pretty clear, 1 light-year is the distance that light covers in one year.

From this last sentence you can see the confusion that sometimes might arise around it. Effectively, when you are looking at the stars you are looking through time, because the light (which is the actual image, let’s say, of that star) has been travelling for 3, 4 or maybe 800 years before coming to us. So, if we wanted to explain it with a photographic analogy, when we look at particular objects in the Sky, we are actually looking at old pictures, as old as they are distant from us, and that’s because even something as fast as light takes a long time to arrive here. This is how large the Universe is.


How fast is the speed of light?

299,792,458 m/s.
That's 7.5 complete circles around the Equator every second!


Parsec

Now this one is a little bit more complicated because it deals with geometry and trigonometry. You definitely do not need to know all that to understand what a parsec is. As before, it is a unit of length and it corresponds to approximately 3.26 lightyears.

The way it is measured is different and it makes use of an effect called parallax, the name comes from parallax at arcsecond. Parallax is a displacement or difference in the apparent position of an object viewed along two different lines of sight and is measured by the angle of inclination between those two lines.

It is quite difficult to visualise, but bear in mind that the Earth rotates around the Sun in 365 days. Now, we are looking at a star and we want to take note of its position at the same time of everyday of the year. Since the Earth moves, the star will show a slight movement, as described by the parallax effect, by changing line of sight. This line is an imaginary line that goes from Earth, our place of observation, through a “fixed” near object, in this case the Sun, and connects the star, the final object of our observation. This movement is measured by the angle of this different lines of sight. 1 parsec happens when in one year the angle is equal to 1 arcsecond, namely 1/3600 of a degree.

Finally, this is quite an advanced topic and it requires a bit of geometrical knowledge but what you need to know is that is a measure of length and can be used sometimes when lightyears don’t suffice.


Spectral Type

Among the various concepts here presented this might be the longest, and difficult to understand. The reason is that there are many types of classifications, and they depend on different aspects, different scientists etc. You might need to be familiar with basic physics concepts such as wavelength, electro-magnetism and diffraction. However, in this brief description I will try to lay it out as simply as possible.

Let’s start from what a star is. Basically, a star is a gaseous object that consumes, or “burns” certain chemical elements to produce energy. This energy is vital to maintain its temperature, mass and volume and its overall density. Depending on the element that nuclear reactions use and produce, our star can have different characteristics in terms of its color, energy radiations, and spectrum meaning what elements and wavelength it emits. It’s also important to remember that speaking of energy is equal to temperature, indicating surface temperature.

All these characteristics belong to the outermost shell of a star, since it’s quite impossible to see what goes on inside (why? Well, it’s too bright) although physics can help. This shell is called the photosphere, the layer from which light is radiated.

Most stars are classified under the Morgan-Keenan (MK) system which uses letters O, B, A, F, G, K and M in this order from hottest (O type) to coolest (M type). Further description sees the use of numbers from 0, being hottest, to 9, being coolest (for example, A9, F0 etc.). Another parameter is added to indicate a star’s luminosity class which approximately indicates its mass, it distinguishes giants from dwarves. It is indicated with Roman numbers (I-II-III etc.). Ia+ are hypergiants, while VII are white dwarfs.

As an example, let’s look at the Sun with a spectral class of G2V (which is read G-two-fifth). G2 points to a relatively cooler star with a surface temperature of 5.800K, and V indicates a main-sequence star, namely not too large not too small.

What does main-sequence mean? It refers to a very important diagram in astronomy and astrophysics, namely the Hertzsprung-Russel diagram (HR). This diagram represents all the stars in our galaxy, but it could also be drawn for other galaxies, with the absolute magnitude on the Y-axis and the spectral type on the X-axis. What this diagram shows is that there seems to be a well-defined pattern of stars from small and cool, to larger and hotter. This pattern is called main sequence and 99% of the stars are included in this trend. Outside of it there are rare specimen of stars which represent the extremes. For example, hypergiants that are insanely huge but can also be cool, or white dwarfs that are relatively small but scorching hot. The HR diagram is the closest thing to a galaxy picture. As a matter of fact, HR diagrams for other galaxies exist and by analysing their main sequence, information such as the age and most importantly the evolution stage of that galaxy can be inferred. In fact, newer galaxies have a sparser trend, with no defined main sequences, on the other hand older systems have predominant “dying stars” which are on the brink of extinction. How do you measure that? Energy is a determining factor: the average temperatures tend to be lower, and the material is scarce. Our galaxy seems to be somewhere in the middle.

One last concept that is inherently connected to the spectral type is the color of a star. If you remember from your student days when the teacher was speaking of electromagnetism and wavelength, there is a tight connection between energy and wavelength. The higher the energy, or temperature, the shorter the wavelength. When we talk about light and what’s visible, we are actually referring to a particular range of wavelengths called, intuitively, the visible spectrum. This range goes from 350nm to 750nm (nm indicate nanometers which is 1/1000 of a millimeter). Now, if you try and recall that spectrum you can see that it goes from red, longer wavelengths, to blue, shorter wavelength. This trend works exactly in the same way for stars. They emit a bunch of radiations on every possible spectrum: x-rays, infrared, ultraviolet, gamma rays, cosmic rays and, more importantly for stargazers, visible spectrum rays. We perceive these radiations as colour, and since energy = temperature = wavelength, hotter stars will appear blueish or even white, whereas cooler stars will appear reddish, yellow or orange. This might slightly change due to an effect called “Doppler Shift” which depends on the velocity and overall motion of an object compared to Earth, namely if it moves away from us the effect will lengthen the waves shifting the colour to red, while if it moves towards us the wavelength will be shorten and the shift will be toward the blue side. This concept is not trivial in astronomy because, first of all it tells you that all the objects in the Universe are moving but also because perfectly visible stars could become invisible if this effect moves their dominant radiation to a non-visible spectrum such as infrared.

Sometimes, on this website, you’ll see the Morgan-Keenan notation accompanied by either bin, doub, mul, or tri. What do they mean? Although, it’s not part of the actual classification, I wanted to include such information because when observing the sky, certain stars might appear having variable spectral types (in terms of color, luminosity etc.). Bin indicates binary systems which means 2 stars orbiting around each other (or a common center of gravity), doub indicates double stars and the main difference is that they are not close to each other, they just appear double when looked at, sometimes star A is in front, others is star B, or maybe the brightness of one totally outshines the other. Tri as you might have guessed indicates triple, and mul multiple star systems.

As far as we know, binary and triple systems are actual the norm, rather than the exception in the Universe. As a matter of fact, the Solar System can be considered a statistical anomaly, luckily for us. Although, if you observe the planets in our system you might notice something quite peculiar. Earth is a solid planet, meaning it has a solid surface divided in layers (crust, mantle, core etc). There are other planets like Earth, Mars for example, or Venus and Mercury. Looking further we have Jupiter. Now that is a strange looking planet. First of all, it is completely gaseous and has a density lower than water’s. In addition, it is massive, larger than all the other planets combined. Well, as it turns out Jupiter is actually a failed, or aborted star. It means that it was supposed to be a star but, in the process, it didn’t reach the temperatures or pressures needed to trigger the nuclear reactions that ultimately result in the genesis of a star. So again, we have been pretty lucky because Jupiter is stunningly beautiful, and also strategically important when it comes to outside threats.


Zenith

To put it simply the zenith in astronomical observation is the highest point in the celestial sphere which corresponds with a vertical position to the observer. It’s the direction directly above our heads, when you look up from where you stand you are looking at the zenith. The opposite direction is called nadir, but since in observation the objects along the nadir direction are covered by the Earth itself is not very important.



The Telescope


Let’s start from the book definition of a telescope: the telescope is an optical instrument using lenses, curved mirrors, or a combination of both, to observe distant objects. The history behind it is not important in this case, although it is important to recall all the remarkable upgrades and scientific breakthroughs that allowed us today to enjoy stunning pictures of our, as well as other, galaxies.

As previously mentioned, light is divided in an electromagnetic spectrum. What we see and perceive as colour is called the visible spectrum which is just a very small part of it all. Extremely advanced telescopes can now see other radiations such as gamma ray, x-rays, infrared, ultraviolet and some particular telescopes can go down to radio waves (which are the longest) so called radio-telescopes, that do not really see but hear the sound of the Universe. For example, Arecibo Observatory in Puerto Rico is one of the biggest radio-telescopes out there, unfortunately it has been put out of service quite recently.

Despite all this, for stargazers the only tool available that can be within a normal person’s budget is an optical telescope. There is plenty of models, sizes, types and they have different perks, pros and cons that you should evaluate based upon your intentions about observation. Keep in mind the 2 most important properties of a telescope: first, its ability to gather light. The better light can be gathered the further and clearer you can see. It usually depends on the aperture of the device and you want a minimum of 90mm for refractors or 130mm for reflectors. Do not get obsessed with these terms they only indicate how light is gathered, either through lenses (refractor) or through mirrors (reflectors). Secondly, the magnification which refers to how much larger the telescope can make objects appear. Magnification, strictly speaking, is not an attribute of the telescope it mainly depends on the eyepiece being used

When purchasing you might see the term Focal Length. It basically refers to the actual length of the telescope, remember that a shorter focal length gives a wider field of view, but the objects will appear smaller. On the other hand, a longer focal length will give you larger images but also narrower portions of the Sky. Although, these aspects are quite secondary for us because it depends on your particular budget and purposes.

What I’d like you to focus is the type of mount for your telescope, that could be quite a steep investment, but it will certainly help you in localising stars and more importantly to follow them while they move through the Sky.

The first type is called altazimuth mount and is a simple 2-axis mount for supporting and rotating the telescope around 2 perpendicular axes, this is pretty much standard with 99% of the telescopes out there. One axis is vertical and allows movements that change the azimuth (left and right), basically the angle between our line of observation and North. The other axis is horizontal (up and down) and allow us to change the altitude of the pointing direction. This type of mount is generally found in beginners’ telescopes or is the standard for other more advanced ones. However, just keep in mind that this mount allows 2 movements: Up and Down (regulating the altitude) and Left and Right (changing the azimuth).

The second type is a bit more advanced and pricier. It is commonly known as Equatorial mount. It is meant for instruments to compensate for Earth’s rotation by having 1 rotational axis parallel to the Earth’s axis of rotation (the one that Earth rotates around in approximately 24 hours). The advantage of an equatorial mount lies in its ability to allow the instrument attached to it to stay fixed on any celestial object with a diurnal motion (it moves through the Sky during the day) by driving 1 axis at a constant speed, so it matches, thanks to motorised “clock drives” that rotates that axis one revolution every 23 hours and 56 minutes, the Earth’s rotation speed. Some of these mounts may also be equipped with setting circles to allow for the location of objects by their celestial coordinates: Right Ascension and Declination.

For this second type of mounts, observation becomes a bit easier, you just need the celestial coordinates of an object, which are provided in this website, and following the instructions of your particular device you can localise and identify the object you were looking for.

Celestial coordinates are explained in detail further down in this documentation. From here though the question is simple, how do normal people with normal telescopes and no fancy Equatorial motorised mounts can locate an object? Well, first of all it is good practice to get a general understanding of how the Sky and the objects on it move.



Guide to Observation


As everyone knows, the Sun rises in the East, and sets to the West. This indicates that the Earth’s rotation around itself (revolution) happens from west to east, in what you can visualise, keeping east as right and west as left, as a clockwise motion. Since the movement of the Sun and the other objects is apparent, because it’s the Earth that it’s moving, they will all follow the same principle, rise in the E and set to the W.

Now I want you to focus your attention on some particular aspects that some of you may know but they are very important for understanding how astronomy of the Solar System works. The Earth rotates around the Sun in approximately 365 days, it is actually a bit more than that by 6 hours. This means that every year, considering 365 days we “lose” or anticipate the year by 6 hours. Every 4 years we have 24 hours delay on the actual year, which means when the Earth is in the same exact position as it was the previous year in respect to the Sun, therefore we have to catch up by adding 1 extra day, namely the 29th February. Those years are well-known as leap years.

Considering now a 3D space model with only the Earth and the Sun we have a 2D plane that identifies Earth’s orbit around the Sun, namely the Ecliptic plane. In this model there is a kind of unbalance because the Earth’s rotation axis is not perpendicular to the ecliptic but is inclined of 23,5°. This actually creates what we know here on Earth as the season cycle. Why? Well, first of all the rotation axis always remains parallel to itself during the rotation, there are some minor movements, but they are so slow that they are negligible for our purpose. Having this condition there will be times when the Earth “shows” a larger portion to the Sun of the Northern Hemisphere, we call it Boreal summer. The days are longer, and weather is hotter because solar rays hit more directly (with a smaller angle) the atmosphere at Northern latitudes. Other times, the Earth will expose more of the Southern side and we will have Boreal winter, or Austral summer. There is a small portion of the Earth, which is not affected by these changes, namely the Tropics. There, the days, meaning daylight, always have the same length and solar rays slightly varies the angle of incidence to a minimal change along the Equator.

In this model there are 2 points that are extremely important. They are the same but opposite. These points happen when the ecliptic coincides with the celestial equator, which is a plane created by extending the Equator to the celestial sphere. They are called the Vernal Equinox (21st March) and the September Equinox (21st September). When the Earth is in that position, everywhere the duration of the day is equal to the night (12 hours – Equinox from Latin means equal to the night). For astronomy the vernal equinox is fundamental because all the celestial coordinates (right ascension and declination) are actual referred to the object’s position at this time of year.

If you had an Equatorial mount with your telescope, you’d only need to follow the instructions of the telescope to set the right ascension and declination of the object you wanted to observe. For altazimuth mounts is not that easy, and this is where this website might come in handy. In the explore section, you can look up information about constellations, and stars, just look for them in the search bar in the upper left corner. If you notice, below that there is a section called Where to Look which asks for a location. What that does it takes the coordinates of the location to calculate the current position of a star. Among the details you’ll see the where key. It might tell you different things. Usually, the instructions will go like this: 38° to the North and 35° to the East. How do you read this? The first part represents a movement on a North-South line. You just look up from where you are (the zenith) put yourself on a N-S trend and look 38° to the N, there you trace a semi-arc that goes around from E to W. The second part is a bit more complicated. First you need to identify the celestial equator, as we have seen previously it is the projection of the Equator on the celestial sphere. How do I do that? You need to know the latitude of your location (don’t worry it is provided in the response). Now, simply do the math: if you were at the Equator the celestial equator would be right over your head, so 90°. If you move North, you’ll see it slowly moving down towards the south. When you are 30° North of the Equator the celestial line will be 90° – 30° towards the South, so basically 60° looking south. If you move south, well the logic is similar but on the other way so the celestial equator will move to the North. If we are correct, being at the North or South pole should mean seeing the celestial equator right along the horizon all around us.

After this step you’ll see that the celestial equator will trace a semi-arc moving from east to west, these are the anchor points where you want to fix this line. Reading the instructions again the second part tells us to look 35° to the East. So, taking the celestial line we’ve just found we move along it 35° from East to West, once there we move up (north) or down (south) until we cross the line we previously identified on the N-S trend. If everything went well there you should see the star. Consider that the engine makes approximate calculations so you might need to move your observation direction of a few degrees around a circle.

Sometimes, the instructions will be different, and you won’t have any result simply because the star might not be visible from that location, or it might not have risen yet or a star is circumpolar, meaning at that particular latitude is always visible, or above the horizon. You’ll have N-S coordinates anyway but since it doesn’t rise nor set the engine will not be able to compute its position in the Sky. It is definitely something that can and will be improved.



Right Ascension


Right ascension (abbreviated RA; symbol α) is the angular distance of a particular point measured eastward along the celestial equator from the Sun at the March equinox the point in question above the earth.

After reading the Guide to Observation you should be familiar with all of these terms. Right Ascension is not an easy concept to grasp first of all if you see its unit of measure, you’ll notice it is in hours/minutes/seconds like this: 02h 31m 48,7s.

Now, how could that be a measure for an angle? It actually isn’t. In contrast, it indicates a time, a very specific time and it refers to the delay a particular star has to the Sun on the vernal equinox. What do I mean for delay? I mean, when the star will rise to the east, even though not all stars will rise because some are always upon the horizon and others are always below. If you wanted, you could convert this into degrees. A complete circle is 360°, the Earth completes a revolution in 24 hours that means that 360° divided 24 h is 15° per hour. Therefore, 2 hours and some minutes is approximately 30°.

Why put that in hours then, couldn’t they just give it in degrees, like normal human beings? It turns out that considering the time gives more information because you know the delay compared to the Sun and other stars and when you learn a bit more you could even infer the best time for observation of that star.

Let’s see for example a star that has a right ascension of 12h 37m 42,2s. This datum means that on the March equinox (21st March) this star will rise 12 hours after the Sun. This tells you that it is clearly visible in March because it rises when the Sun supposedly sets. On the other hand, a star with a right ascension of 01h 08m 34,5s in March rises almost at the same time with the Sun, this doesn’t give any observer the chance to take a look at that star.

Are we condemned to never see that star then? Absolutely not, because as the seasons progress so does the vernal equinox, therefore the Sky “changes”. I’d like you to understand that the Sky doesn’t actually change, but since the Earth moves, you’ll see fixed reference points on the celestial sphere moving. From the vernal equinox the Sun will rises earlier and earlier in the Northern Hemisphere. The stars won’t do so because they are too distant, and we are not orbiting around them. This gives us a great advantage because a star that has a 1-hour delay after 1 month will have a 3-hour delay, the Earth moved. Why did it lose 2 hours? It goes as before, a complete orbit around the Sun is 360° or 24 hours (keep in mind the 15° per hour) in 365 days, or 12 months means 2 hours every month because Earth moved around the Sun of 30°. This is the fundamental theory of right ascension, every month from the Vernal equinox a star “loses” 2 hours to the Sun, remember that its right ascension intended as the actual coordinate doesn’t change, what does is the delay on the Sun. To go back to our previous example, if we wanted to take a look at that star with a RA of 01h 08m 34,5s we should wait for 4-6 months so that its delay became 14-15 hours. That would guarantee the star will be visible since it won’t be outshined by the sunlight.

Although this website does it automatically, you could try and calculate the current delay of a star starting from its right ascension. You just subtract 2 hours for every month from March. For example, let’s take an actual star: Sirius in the Canis Major constellation. Its RA is 6h 45m. This means that on 21st March it will be 6 hours behind the Sun, so when the Sun sets, 6 hours times 15° per hour is 90°, Sirius should be exactly at 90° from the east on the celestial equator (don’t forget the declination that we’ll see in a bit). After 3 months, in June, its delay will be 0 because 3 months times 2 hours a month gives us 6 hours. This means Sirius will rise with the Sun and set with it, we are out of luck. Fast forward by 2 months in August and you’ll see that Sirius now has -4 hours. What does negative delay mean? It means that the star would rise before the Sun, in this case 4 hours before, hence if we wanted to take a look at Sirius in August, we should set the alarm clock and wake up quite early or not even go to bed. If the Sun rises at 5 AM, Sirius will be up from 1 AM.

I took Sirius as an example because it is the brightest star in the Sky, apart from the Sun, and if you didn’t want to stay up late but being comfortable with your partner at 8 or 9 o’clock at night stargazing and trying to see Sirius, you should wait for January or February.



Declination


Declination (abbreviated dec; symbol δ) is the angle measured north or south of the celestial equator, along the hour circle passing through the point in question.

This coordinate is the second fundamental parameter to keep in mind in astronomy. It is easier to understand, and it could be assimilated to latitude for geographical coordinates. So, latitude is the angle between the Equator and a location. When you take a look at London’s latitude (N51°…) it says that the angle between London and the Equator is 51°.

Instead of the actual Equator, in astronomy we consider a celestial equator, we have already seen what that is. A brief reminder: the celestial equator is the projection of the Equator onto the celestial sphere. The declination of a star simply indicates the angle between the celestial equator and the star itself along the meridian, or the N-S line.

How is that helpful for observation? The celestial equator basically represents the Equator, so your location’s latitude can be traced on the celestial sphere. As for London (N51°) just identify the celestial equator and move North, along the meridian, of 51°. There you’ll have a semi-arc parallel to the celestial equator that represents the projection of your latitude. If a star has a declination of 10° it means that its angle from the celestial equator is 10° to the north. Where should I look then? Simply doing the math, we are at 51° north, the star is at 10° north, 10 – 51 is equal to -41 so from the zenith (look in this page for the definition) we move down, because the result is negative, of 41°. To give you a simple formula you can apply when stargazing, the position along the meridian of a star is its angle of declination minus your position’s latitude (δ - θ). Positive angles of declination indicate positions above the Equator and negative ones, below the Equator.

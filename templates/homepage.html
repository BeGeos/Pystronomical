Now, before starting to explore the sky it is paramount that you know what a constellations and stars are, plus some technical stuff that will help you in your observation and overall comprehension.


Constellations

As defined canonically, a constellation is an area of the celestial sphere in which a group of visible stars forms a perceived outline or pattern, typically representing an animal, mythological person or creature, or an inanimate object. The origins of constellations go back in ancient prehistoric time, as well as more recent times, and across cultures. It is not uncommon to see different names for groups of stars in the sky that represent the same constellation in different cultures. For example, the Big Dipper (part of Ursa Major as properly defined) is well known across different cultures where it assumes many names. In western civilisations, among the Greek, for example, it was known as "The Bear", while Latin used to call them "Septem Triones" (the Seven Oxes). Moreover, in Asia it was known as "The Northern Dipper" or elsewhere "The Big Rudder".

Nowadays, a big consensus has been reached upon their names, to eliminate confusion and create a standard nomenclature. There is an organisation that is now in charge when it comes to matters of this sort, either identifying new or ancient constellations, and officialise established ones. This organisation is the International Astronomical Union, or IAU. This is quite an important acronim that you are going to see frequently when looking for celestial objects in manuals, books or online. In 1922, they formally accepted the modern list of 88 constellations, as we know now, and traced, few years later, official constellation boundaries that together cover the entire celestial sphere, namely the sky.

There is a confusing concept that might buffle some of you. Sometimes you hear constellation, other times asterism. So what is the difference? Is there any? Are they the same thing? Well, yes, but no.
A constellation is an official group of stars that has a defined area in the sky and that has been formally accepted. On the other hand, an asterism does not have a formal definition but form a commonly accepted pattern of stars, which represents a part of a constellation (usually, the most recognisable part). For example, the aforementioned Big Dipper has a very well-known shape. Some might see a spoon others a rudder, but what is important is that it is actually not the Ursa Major constellation, it is part of it, probably the most recognisible part of it in the Norther Hemispere, with its 7 bright stars. However, Ursa Major extends well beyond those 7 stars. On average, constellations have between 70 to 80 stars, plus other celestial objects some of which are entire galaxies (so-called Messier objects).

One thing to remember when observing constellations is that the Earth is "divided" in 2 hemispheres. The Northern Hemisphere covers the surface from the Equator up to the North Pole (latitute N 90°), while the Southern Hemisphere extends from the equator to the South Pole (latitude S 90°). Why is this important? As it is commonly accepted (more or less) the Earth orbits around the Sun, a journey that takes 365 days to be complete, and it also circles around itself, specifically around an axis called, very creatively, the rotation axis.
This rotation happens from West to East, which is the reason the Sun appears from the East first. This movement gives us the day, meaning the actual 24 hours that mark a day on Earth. Obviously, this entails various things such as sunrise that starts the hours of daylight, and sunset which in turn introduces the night hours. Now, the reason why we see the Sun "moving", is because we (Earth) rotate around it, and also because we are relatively close to it.

In contrast, the Earth doesn't rotate around other stars, and they are also extremely distant. This means that the night sky never changes, at least to the human eye. This creates a divide: constellations that are only visible from the Northern Hemisphere, and others only from the Southern Hemisphere.

This shouldn't really be a problem, yes if you leave in Sydney you might never get to see Camelopardalis, as well as a Danish nightcrawler will never be able to see Corona Australis, but I can assure you that there is plenty to see anyway wherever you are in the world.

Previously, we talked about stars. It would be better to give a little explanation about what they are, and why they are so important.


Stars

Let's start from the simplest definition, by the book: a star is an astronomical object consisting of a luminous spheroid of plasma held together by its own gravity. Got it? Well, maybe you did but this is not very clear. So let's rewind it a little bit.

I want to start with a fairly good example of a star which can be understood very easily. The Sun is the nearest star to Earth and it is actually the main glue that holds the Solar System together. It protects us from cosmic radiations from outer space, its gravitational pull helps maintaining bigger celestial objects at bay, such as asteroids and potential threats to our planet. When you look at the Sun, not directly of course, which is something you never should do, what do you see? Do you happen to see a yellowish rounded ball traveling through the sky? If so congratulations, you have just seen how stars are.

They all have spherical shapes, they are mostly gaseous and have very low densities and what happens when you have something very "soft" around a strong center of gravity? It collapses and form the simplest shape and almost the more convenient in terms of surface energy, namely a sphere. Of course, not every star is perfectly spherical, but in those cases there are other forces acting upon it, for example centrifugal or centripetal forces. Neutron stars rotate around themselves thousands of time a second, so it's understandable they might have a bit of an oblungate shape. However, none of that matters for observation, what does is their size, their color and, more importantly, their brightness, term which is refered to as magnitude when applied to celestial objects.

Let's see how and why this 3 aspects are fundamental for observation. You can see more detailed descriptions in the How To Observe section, here I will try to keep it simple and brief.

First of all, size affects the way we see different stars. It is quite easy to understand that a bigger object can be seen better, even at greater distances. This is related to their luminosity, which is the amount of energy stars can emit, obviously a bigger surface emits more energy per surface unit. In the night sky there are many visible stars that are very very very far away. Nonetheless, when the sun goes down , there they are, some of them are even the brightest. This is mainly due to their gargantuan size. Take Rigel for example, in the Orion constellation. Rigel is approximately 860 lightyears away from the Solar System, although it is the 7th brightest star in the night sky. This is mainly due to the fact that Rigel is almost 23 times as massive as the Sun and 300,000 times as luminous. As you can tell, size matters in this case.

Secondly, color. The color is heavily dependent on their chemistry, which means the element stars are consuming, which type of emissions on the visible spectrum. If you are interested you could investigate on what a spectroscopy is. To put it simply, the spectrometer is a tool to understand which elements are present in an object, these elements will let certain radiations pass and absorbe others. Some of this radiations have wavelengths on the visible spectrum. Based on what the object absores and emits, ta dam, we have COLOR. When observing stars in the sky it is quite useful to know what color they have, in technical terms it is called spectral type, which includes a lot of pieces of information other than just the color. Just come back to our favourite star Rigel, it has a spectral type B8. Now, it is not important what it means precisely, just know that a B-type star will appear blueish, depending on the surface temperature of said star. Imagine you are gazing through the night sky, suddendly you see a familiar shape, 3 stars very close to each other forming a sort of a belt (let's face it you need a lot of imagination to enjoy it). Voilà, that's Orion's belt (a common asterism), which means that you are looking at the constellation of Orion (the Giant). You look around and see 2 very bright stars, one is brighter than the other and red, the second is blue. You know Rigel is in there and it is extremely bright, you also know that it emits blueish light, hence it must be the upper left one you saw (in the Southern Hemisphere Orion appears upside down, Rigel would be on the right bottom corner then). That saved you the humiliation to mistake Betelgeuse (the giant's foot) with Rigel (the giant's shoulder). As you can see, color is quite important.

Finally, probably the most important aspect to consider when observing the night sky is a star's magnitude. Simply put magnitude indicates a star's brightness, but there are 2 types of magnitude. Absolute Magnitude and Apparent Magnitude. In observation, only the latter is useful. For further explanation, please take a look at the dedicated section (How to Observe - Nomenclature). Magnitude directly relates to how bright, therefore visible, a star is. Although, magnitude is not exclusive of stars, every celestial object has an apparent magnitude, either it emits light, or reflects it. One confusing aspect of this it's that the lower the magnitude, the more visible the object is. This has historical reasons but bear in mind this concept.

Magnitude values can be also negative. For example, the Moon has an apparent magnitude of -19. Just to give a little context here, the brightest star in the sky, a part from the Sun, is Sirius (in the Canis Major constellation). Sirius has an apparent magnitude of -1.47.

However, observation is an awesome activity that could just be carried out with a few simple concepts.
